2026 CMS Care Management Program Selector

Medicare pays for structured care management beyond the visit. This tool maps each program, who qualifies, who bills, what it pays, and how LucidAct operationalizes it—under the CY 2026 Physician Fee Schedule.
CY 2026 Care Management Overview

Effective January 1, 2026, CMS protected all time-based care management codes—RPM, RTM, CCM, BHI/CoCM, and related G-codes—as designated care-management services, exempting them from the new “efficiency adjustment” that cuts work RVUs on many E/M codes. Care management revenue holds while some visit-based payments decline. See the CMS CY 2026 PFS Fact Sheet and the AMA 2026 PFS overview.

FQHCs and RHCs: most care management services—CCM, BHI, RPM/RTM-equivalent work, and new social-needs codes—roll up to G0511 as a single monthly payment rather than individual CPT codes. For details, see the CodingIntel RHC/FQHC update and the CMS Physician Fee Schedule page.

Three headline changes for 2026: new partial-month codes for RPM and RTM (covering 2–15 day engagement), modestly higher rates on core management codes, and expanded SDOH/navigation G-codes that stack cleanly with CCM, PCM, APCM, and BHI. See: Medsien – 2026 Remote Care Changes, Prevounce – 2026 PFS FAQs, ChartSpan – 2026 PFS Care‑Management Highlights.

What is RPM?
RPM uses connected devices to collect patient vitals outside the office—blood pressure, weight, glucose, pulse oximetry—and bill monthly for device supply and clinical management time. In 2026, new partial-month codes make RPM billable even when engagement is lower than the legacy 16-day threshold. See: CMS 2026 PFS Fact Sheet, RPMlogix 2026 Final Rule overview, Prevounce 2026 RPM FAQs.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patient with at least one acute or chronic condition where vitals data will guide treatment.
- Practitioner order and documented care plan on file.
- Medical-grade device that auto-collects and transmits data (practice- or vendor-supplied).
- Minimum transmission days: 16+ days → bill 99454; 2–15 days → bill new 99445 (2026).
- RPM time must be distinct from CCM, PCM, or TCM time in the same month—no double-counting.
See: EHI 2026 RPM Updates, RPMlogix 2026 Final Rule, Prevounce 2026 FAQs.
Who May Bill?
Physicians, NPs, and PAs. Clinical staff may perform most activities under general supervision. One practitioner per patient per month; the device-owning entity bills supply codes. IDTFs may not bill RPM. See: EHI RPM updates, RPMlogix guide.
Billing Structure
RPM stacks across four layers each month:
  • Setup (99453) – One-time enrollment, device setup, and patient education.
  • Device supply – 99454 (16–30 days); 99445 NEW (2–15 days for partial-month engagement).
  • Management time – 99457 (first 20 min/mo); 99458 (each additional 20 min); 99470 NEW (10–19 min when full threshold not met).
  • Standalone data review (99091) – 30 min of practitioner-only data review when not counted toward other codes.
Billable management time covers data review, care-plan adjustments, patient/caregiver communication, and specialist coordination—documented and not double-counted. See: RPMlogix 2026 Final Rule summary, Prevounce RPM FAQs.

What is RTM?
RTM tracks non-physiologic data—medication adherence, therapy device use, pain, functional status—for patients with musculoskeletal, respiratory, or other qualifying conditions. Focused on therapeutic response and self-reported outcomes, not vitals. In 2026, CMS added partial-month supply and management codes and clarified stacking with other programs. See: Telehealth Resource Center CY 2026 RPM/RTM update, Medsien 2026 remote care changes, Prevounce 2026 PFS FAQs.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patient with a qualifying condition (commonly MSK or respiratory) where therapeutic adherence or function can be monitored remotely.
- Device or app ordered by a physician, QHP, or eligible therapist capturing self-reported non-physiologic data.
- Minimum logged use: 16+ days → bill 98976; 2–15 days → bill new 98985 (2026).
- RTM time must be distinct from RPM and other care management—use RTM or RPM for a given condition, not both. See: Telehealth Resource Center, Medsien, Prevounce FAQs.
Who May Bill?
Physicians, QHPs, and eligible therapists (PT, OT) who order and oversee the service. Clinical and therapy staff may perform activities under appropriate supervision; the billing provider owns data review and treatment decisions. See: Telehealth Resource Center CY 2026 RTM, Medsien RTM overview.
Billing Structure
RTM stacks across four layers, mirroring RPM but focused on therapeutic outcomes:
  • Setup (98975) – One-time device/app onboarding and patient education.
  • Device/software supply – 98976 (16–30 days); 98985 NEW (2–15 days).
  • Management time – 98980 (first 20+ min/mo); 98981 (each additional 20 min).
  • Short-duration management (98979) NEW – 10–19 minutes when the full 20-minute threshold is not reached.
Billable management time covers data review, adherence assessment, patient/caregiver communication, and treatment adjustments—documented and not double-counted. See: Telehealth Resource Center RTM webinar, Prevounce FAQs.
Workflow
1
Onboarding
  • Identify eligible patients: post-surgical MSK, chronic low back pain, COPD/asthma
  • Obtain consent and prescribe the RTM app or device
  • Train patient on logging symptoms, activity, and adherence
2
Tracking Period
  • Patient logs therapy activity, symptoms, and medication use
  • Target 2–15 days → bill 98985
  • Target 16+ days → bill 98976
3
Review & Support
  • Review data for 10, 20, or 40+ min per month
  • Document interventions and coaching notes
  • Adjust the care plan or home exercise program
  • Escalate for flares or non-response

What is CCM?
CCM pays for the between-visit work on patients with 2+ chronic conditions—medication management, care coordination, and structured monthly outreach. Billed monthly based on documented clinical staff time. See: CMS CCM FAQ, ChartSpan – What is CCM?, AMA CCM explainer.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patient with 2+ chronic conditions expected to last 12+ months (or until death), placing the patient at significant risk of death, acute exacerbation, or functional decline.
- Comprehensive, person-centered care plan documented in a certified health record and shared with the patient/caregiver.
- Documented patient consent (verbal or written); only one practitioner bills CCM per month. See: CMS CCM FAQ, ChartSpan CCM guide.
Who May Bill?
Physicians, NPs, PAs, CNS, and other QHPs. Clinical staff perform most work under general supervision; the billing practitioner directs and owns the care plan. One practitioner per patient per month. CCM may stack with RPM, BHI, and other services when time and requirements are distinct. Details: CMS CCM FAQ, AMA CCM.
Billing Structure
CCM is tiered by monthly clinical staff time:
  • 99490 – Standard CCM: 20+ min clinical staff/mo.
  • 99439 – Add-on: each additional 20 min (used with 99490).
  • 99487 – Complex CCM: 60+ min clinical staff with moderate/high MDM and care plan revision.
  • 99489 – Complex add-on: each additional 30 min.
  • 99491 – CCM by practitioner: 30+ min personally performed by physician or QHP (no clinical staff time counted).
Billable time includes phone calls, portal messages, medication reconciliation, coordination, and care-plan updates—documented and not double-counted. Good code‑level summaries: ChartSpan 2026 PFS CCM analysis, CMS Physician Fee Schedule look‑up.
Workflow
1
Enrollment
  • Identify eligible patients from panels or risk lists
  • Obtain patient consent
  • Build initial care plan: problem list, goals, and self-management tasks
2
Monthly Management
  • Conduct structured outreach, medication checks, and symptom assessments
  • Close care gaps
  • Document cumulative minutes toward 99490/99439 or 99487/99489
3
Care Plan Review & Escalation
  • Billing practitioner reviews CCM notes and updates the care plan
  • Escalate to RPM, RTM, TCM, APCM, or in-person care when red flags appear

What is PCM?
PCM is CCM for a single serious condition. Where CCM covers patients with multiple chronic conditions, PCM applies when one driving diagnosis—advanced heart failure, severe asthma, complex diabetes—requires focused, ongoing management by a single responsible clinician for at least three months. See: AMA Principal Care Management overview, ChartSpan PCM guide.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patient with one serious chronic condition expected to last 3+ months, placing the patient at significant risk of hospitalization, acute exacerbation, or functional decline.
- Clinician develops and maintains a disease-specific care plan and provides ongoing management.
- Patient consent required. One clinician per patient per month; PCM and CCM generally do not bill together for the same clinician and patient in the same month. See: CMS CCM/PCM FAQ, AMA PCM.
Who May Bill?
Physicians, NPs, PAs, and other QHPs managing the patient’s qualifying condition. Clinical staff perform most activities under general supervision with time counted toward PCM minutes. One practitioner per patient per month—the clinician primarily responsible for the principal condition. Sources: AMA PCM guidance, ChartSpan PCM billing.
Billing Structure
Two codes, tiered by monthly clinical staff time:
  • 99426 – PCM: 30+ min clinical staff/mo directed by a physician or QHP.
  • 99427 – Add-on: each additional 30 min.
Billable time covers medication management, provider coordination, patient/caregiver communication, and care-plan updates—documented and not double-counted against CCM or other time-based codes. Good breakdowns: ChartSpan PCM guide, CMS PFS look‑up.
Workflow
1
Assessment & Enrollment
  • Identify patients with one serious high-risk condition where your team is the primary manager
  • Obtain patient consent
  • Build a focused care plan: goals, medications, and monitoring needs
2
Monthly PCM Management
  • Conduct structured outreach, symptom and adherence checks
  • Perform medication reconciliation
  • Document at least 30 min/mo for 99426
  • Add 99427 increments as needed
3
Care Review & Transition
  • Billing clinician reviews PCM notes and adjusts treatment
  • Stay in PCM, or transition to CCM when multiple conditions emerge
  • Add RPM/RTM for closer monitoring if indicated

What is TCM?
TCM covers the 30 days post-discharge. It bundles medication reconciliation, a timed face-to-face visit, and provider coordination into a single payment—two codes that differ by visit timing and decision-making complexity. See: CMS TCM Fact Sheet, AAFP – Transitional Care Management.
Patient Eligibility
- Discharged from an eligible inpatient or observation setting (acute hospital, psychiatric hospital, LTCH, SNF, IRF, or outpatient observation/partial hospitalization).
- Returning to community setting—home, domiciliary, rest home, or assisted living; not to another inpatient facility.
- Billing practitioner assumes responsibility for the 30-day TCM episode, including required non-face-to-face services and a face-to-face visit within 7 or 14 days.
- One practitioner bills TCM per discharge episode; no overlap with conflicting services. See: CMS TCM Fact Sheet.
Who May Bill?
Physicians, NPs, PAs, CNS, and other QHPs with Medicare billing privileges. Clinical staff perform non-face-to-face work under general supervision. One practitioner per discharge episode. TCM generally does not co-bill with CCM/PCM in the same month for the same practitioner and patient unless time and documentation are clearly distinct. See: CMS TCM Fact Sheet.
Billing Structure
  • 99495 – Moderate complexity TCM: Patient/caregiver contact within 2 business days of discharge; moderate MDM; face-to-face visit within 14 days.
  • 99496 – High complexity TCM: Same 2-business-day contact; high MDM; face-to-face visit within 7 days.
The 30-day TCM period runs from discharge through day 29. All elements must be documented to support the billed code. For detailed examples, see: CMS MLN TCM guide.
Workflow
1
Outreach & Discharge Review
  • Receive discharge notification
  • Review discharge summary and medication list
  • Contact patient or caregiver within 2 business days
  • Assess needs and schedule the follow-up visit
2

  • Complete the visit within 7 days (99496) or 14 days (99495)
  • Perform medication reconciliation
  • Assess symptoms and functional status
  • Update the care plan
3

  • Provide phone/portal follow-up throughout the 30-day window
  • Coordinate tests and referrals
  • Monitor for red flags
  • Enroll into CCM, PCM, RPM, or APCM before day 30

What is APCM?
APCM bundles chronic care, preventive services, behavioral health, and social-needs support into a comprehensive per-patient monthly payment—paying for population management, not just visits. In 2026, operationalized through expanded G-codes for community health integration, principal illness navigation, and peer support layered on top of primary care. Aligned with ACO and advanced primary care models. See: Hall Render 2026 PFS highlights, ChartSpan 2026 PFS summary.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patient attributed to a participating primary care practice or FQHC/RHC using advanced primary care or ACO-aligned G-codes.
- Higher-risk or complex patients benefiting from multidisciplinary teams, community health workers, and navigation support.
- Comprehensive care plan with defined team roles (clinicians, CHWs, navigators, peer support) per applicable G-codes.
- Practice meets documentation and supervision requirements for each code and applicable payment model (ACO REACH, MSSP, etc.). Sources: Hall Render 2026 overview, ChartSpan.
Who May Bill?
Primary care physicians, NPs, PAs, and FQHC/RHC practitioners who meet each G-code's requirements. CHW/navigator services (G0019, G0022, G0023, G0024) billed by the supervising practitioner under general supervision. Peer support codes (G0140) integrate trained peers under practitioner oversight. In FQHCs/RHCs, payments roll up to G0511 with ACO attribution and performance incentives layered on top. See: CodingIntel RHC/FQHC update, CMS Physician Fee Schedule.
Billing Structure
APCM combines a monthly care management payment with targeted G-codes for social-needs and navigation work:
  • G0511 – General care management (FQHC/RHC): monthly payment encompassing CCM, BHI, and other care management services.
  • G0019 – Community health integration: 60 min/mo CHW services for SDOH, coordination, and resource linkage.
  • G0022 – Community health integration add-on: each additional 30 min.
  • G0023 – Principal illness navigation: 60 min/mo for patients with serious conditions.
  • G0024 – Principal illness navigation add-on: each additional 30 min.
  • G0140 – Peer support navigation: 60 min/mo peer specialist services for engagement and self-management.
These stack on top of standard E/M and care management services and blend with prospective or performance-based payments in APCM/ACO arrangements. Good breakdowns of the new and expanded G‑codes: Hall Render 2026 PFS highlights, ChartSpan.

  • Execute referrals and SDOH interventions
  • Document time by role and G-code (G0511, G0019/G0022, G0023/G0024, peer support)

  • Embed BHI/CoCM where clinically indicated

What is AWV?
The AWV is an annual preventive visit covered 100% under Part B—structured health risk assessment, history review, risk-factor screening, and a personalized prevention plan. It is also the primary touchpoint for standardized SDOH screening (G0136). See: CMS AWV Quick Reference, CMS Preventive Services.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare Part B beneficiaries enrolled for 12+ months, not within 12 months of an IPPE (G0402) or prior AWV.
- G0438 (initial) billed once per lifetime; G0439 (subsequent) annually.
- SDOH screening (G0136) may be added when clinically appropriate, generally no more than once every 6 months. Details: CMS AWV chart.
Who May Bill?
Physicians, NPs, PAs, CNS, and other CMS-permitted practitioners. Clinical staff may complete many HRA components under supervision; the billing practitioner reviews findings and finalizes the prevention plan. G0136 co-billed by the same practitioner when requirements are met; cost sharing generally waived with modifier-33. See: CMS SDOH Risk Assessment guidance (SE24006).
Billing Structure
Three billable elements:
  • G0438 – Initial AWV: once per lifetime. Comprehensive HRA, history review, provider and medication list, cognitive screening, and written prevention plan.
  • G0439 – Subsequent AWV: annually. Updates HRA and prevention plan; reviews risk factors, functional status, and medications.
  • G0136 – SDOH Risk Assessment: 5–15 min standardized screening for social-risk factors. Billable with AWV, E/M, or BH visits, no more than every 6 months. Cost sharing waived with modifier-33.
AWVs carry no patient cost sharing under Part B. Guidance: CMS AWV Quick Reference, CMS SDOH G0136 memo.

What are BHI & CoCM?
Two tiers of behavioral health integration under Medicare:

BHI (99484): monthly service for patients with 1+ mental/behavioral/psychiatric conditions. Clinical staff provide at least 20 min of behaviorally focused care management under the billing practitioner—assessment, care-plan development, monitoring, and coordination.

CoCM (99492–99494, G2214): higher-intensity team model requiring a behavioral health care manager and psychiatric consultant working alongside the treating practitioner, with a patient registry and structured case reviews. See: CMS MLN909432 – Behavioral Health Integration Services, ThoroughCare 99484 overview, APA – Getting Paid in the Collaborative Care Model.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patient with 1+ mental, behavioral, or psychiatric condition (including SUD) manageable in primary care or collaboratively with BH specialists.
- Condition may be pre-existing or newly diagnosed; use validated scales (PHQ-9, GAD-7) to support documentation.
- Patient requires ongoing management and coordination beyond intermittent visits—mild self-limited symptoms do not qualify.
- BHI: appropriate for mild-moderate or stable BH needs managed in primary care. CoCM: appropriate for moderate-severe, complex, or treatment-resistant conditions requiring psychiatric consultation. Detailed criteria: CMS BHI MLN, Proem 99484 overview.
Who May Bill?
Physicians, NPs, PAs, CNS, and other QHPs providing ongoing primary or specialty care.
BHI: Clinical staff perform most activities under general supervision. A BH care manager is optional but recommended.
CoCM: Requires a defined care triad—treating practitioner, BH care manager (social worker, nurse, or psychologist), and psychiatric consultant providing regular case review via a registry-based caseload.
One practitioner per patient per month for BHI or CoCM. Both may stack with CCM or other services when time and requirements are distinct. See: CMS BHI FAQ, AAFP BHI coding resource.
Billing Structure
BHI (99484)
20+ min/mo of clinical staff time focused on behavioral health: validated scale assessment, care-plan development and revision, proactive follow-up, and provider/caregiver coordination.

CoCM codes:
- 99492 – Initial month: first 70 min of BH care manager time with psychiatric consultant.
- 99493 – Subsequent months: first 60 min.
- 99494 – Add-on: each additional 30 min (billed with 99492 or 99493).
- G2214 – 30-min CoCM option for lower-intensity months; same team and registry requirements.

All CoCM codes require: (1) a patient registry, (2) regular case review between the BH care manager and psychiatric consultant, and (3) documented care-plan adjustments communicated to the treating practitioner. Helpful coding and payment guides: IPRO BHI/CoCM Coding Guide, CodingIntel CoCM in RHC/FQHC.

What is SDOH Screening & Intervention?
SDOH services pay for identifying and addressing social-risk factors—food insecurity, housing instability, transportation barriers, utility insecurity—that drive health outcomes and care access. G0136 pays for standardized screening; 2026 G-codes (CHI, PIN) pay for ongoing navigation and community health worker interventions. All integrate with AWV, primary care, BH, and care management—not standalone. See: CMS SE24006 – SDOH Risk Assessment G0136, Hall Render 2026 PFS highlights.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patient receiving an eligible E/M, BH, or preventive service where SDOH screening will meaningfully inform the care plan—particularly patients with chronic disease, high utilization, or known social-risk indicators.
- For CHI and PIN navigation codes: documented social-risk needs and clinical complexity requiring longitudinal follow-up.
- G0136 frequency limit: no more than once every 6 months. See: CMS SE24006.
Who May Bill?
G0136: physicians, NPs, PAs, CNS, and other practitioners billing eligible E/M or preventive services. CHI/PIN G-codes (G0019, G0022, G0023, G0024): billed by the supervising practitioner; services delivered by trained CHWs or navigators under general supervision. FQHCs/RHCs: SDOH services typically bill under G0511 with SDOH documentation supporting APCM or ACO arrangements. References: CodingIntel RHC/FQHC update, CMS PFS page.
Billing Structure
Two categories of SDOH billing in 2026:
  • SDOH Risk Assessment (G0136): 5–15 min standardized screening for social-risk factors. Billable with AWV, most E/M, and BH services; no more than once every 6 months. Cost sharing waived with modifier-33 as preventive.
  • SDOH Care Management & Navigation:
    • G0019 – Community health integration: 60 min/mo CHW assessment, coaching, and resource linkage.
    • G0022 – CHI add-on: each additional 30 min.
    • G0023 – Principal illness navigation: 60 min/mo for serious-condition patients needing intensive navigation.
    • G0024 – PIN add-on: each additional 30 min.
    In FQHC/RHC and APCM/ACO settings, these codes typically roll up to G0511 while supporting richer SDOH documentation.
Code‑level details: Hall Render 2026 PFS summary, ChartSpan 2026 PFS analysis.

What is GDMT Optimization?
GDMT is the evidence-based drug and device regimen standard for conditions like heart failure, ASCVD, COPD/asthma, and diabetes (per ACC/AHA/HFSA, GOLD, ADA guidelines). There is no single GDMT billing code—GDMT work is funded through CCM, PCM, RPM/RTM, BHI/CoCM, and APCM. This module shows how to operationalize GDMT within those existing payment structures. See: 2022 ACC/AHA/HFSA Heart Failure Guideline, ADA Standards of Care.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patient with a condition where GDMT evidence is strong: HFrEF, ASCVD, T2DM, CKD, COPD/asthma, or post-MI secondary prevention.
- Suboptimal GDMT identified: missing drug classes, under-titrated doses, undocumented contraindications, or poor adherence.
- Engaged and willing to participate in medication adjustments, monitoring, and follow-up; often already in CCM, PCM, RPM/RTM, or APCM.
- HF-focused programs: prioritize HFrEF (LVEF ≤40%) with recent hospitalization, ED visit, or persistent symptoms. See: ACC/AHA/HFSA HF guideline, KFF Medicare & chronic disease.
Who May Bill?
Billed through existing codes. Primary care clinicians, cardiologists, endocrinologists, pulmonologists, and other specialists use CCM, PCM, RPM/RTM, BHI/CoCM, APCM, and TCM codes per each program's requirements. Clinical pharmacists, nurses, and care staff perform titration, education, and adherence support under general supervision with time counted toward applicable codes. Assign billing responsibility explicitly (e.g., CCM + RPM under PCP, PCM under cardiology) to prevent duplicate billing and align with ACO/APCM attribution. For payment policy context: AMA 2026 MPFS Summary, CMS CY 2026 PFS Fact Sheet.
Billing Structure (Using Existing Codes)
Map GDMT work to the right program:
  • CCM / Complex CCM – Multi-morbid patients (HF + diabetes + CKD + COPD) requiring multi-drug titration and monthly non-face-to-face management.
  • PCM – Single dominant condition (HFrEF, brittle T2DM) where one clinician owns GDMT intensification.
  • RPM / RTM – Vitals monitoring (BP, weight, O2 sat) or therapeutic response tracking during titration (HF/HTN via RPM, inhaler use via RTM).
  • BHI / CoCM – When depression, anxiety, SUD, or adherence barriers complicate GDMT uptake.
  • APCM & SDOH – CHWs, navigators, and peers address cost barriers, transportation, and social needs undermining adherence (G0019, G0022, G0023, G0024, G0140).
Each GDMT encounter—titration call, lab follow-up, remote monitoring review—is documented with time allocated to the applicable care-management code. GDMT itself is not billed directly. Overviews of how chronic disease management codes support guideline‑based care: ChartSpan 2026 PFS summary, Prevounce 2026 PFS FAQs.

Program Selection Decision Guide

APCM participating? Yes → APCM is the base; layer RPM/RTM, BHI/CoCM, and TCM. No → route to CCM (2+ chronic conditions) or PCM (one serious condition), then add remote monitoring and behavioral health by clinical need and team capacity.

Choosing Between BHI and CoCM

BHI (≥20 min/mo): lower intensity, standalone, fits mild–moderate BH needs. CoCM (60–70+ min/mo): higher intensity, requires the full three-person team and registry-based case management, reserved for moderate–severe or complex BH conditions.

Continuation Standards: BHI & CoCM

Review continuation monthly. BHI continues while an active BH condition is present, validated scales are in use, a living care plan exists, and 20+ min of documented work is logged. CoCM continues only while the full team and registry are active and 60–70+ min of care manager time remain clinically necessary.

Patient Archetypes & Optimal Program Mix

Five archetypes for playbook and pro forma design: complex primary care (APCM + RPM + CoCM), multi-morbid (complex CCM + RPM + BHI), single high-risk condition (PCM + RTM + BHI), mild BH needs (CCM + RPM + BHI), severe BH needs (complex CCM + RPM + CoCM).

Team Requirements by Program

Required vs optional roles by program: RPM (billing provider + clinical staff + device specialist), RTM (billing provider/therapist + therapy staff), CCM/PCM (billing provider + clinical staff), APCM (multidisciplinary primary care team), TCM (billing provider + clinical staff + case manager), BHI (billing provider + clinical staff), CoCM (required triad: provider + BH care manager + psychiatric consultant).

CCM Service Levels: Regular vs Complex

Regular CCM: 2+ chronic conditions, 20–40 min/mo, low-moderate MDM. Complex CCM: 60+ min/mo consistently, moderate-high MDM, multiple specialists, frequent exacerbations, and high coordination demands.
