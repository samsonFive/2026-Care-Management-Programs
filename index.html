2026 CMS Care Management Program Selector

Care Management programs let teams bill Medicare for structured coordination and ongoing support for patients with chronic or acute needs. These codes support remote monitoring, behavioral health management, transition support, and primary care under the CY 2026 Physician Fee Schedule.
CY 2026 Care Management Overview

Beginning January 1, 2026, Medicare finalized a broad set of updates to care‑management and remote‑care services under the Physician Fee Schedule (PFS). Time‑based care‑management codes—including RPM, RTM, CCM, behavioral health integration (BHI/CoCM), and related G‑codes—are protected as designated care‑management services and are exempt from the new “efficiency adjustment” that reduces work RVUs for many non‑time‑based E/M services. This helps preserve longitudinal care‑management revenue even as some E/M payments face downward pressure. See the CMS CY 2026 PFS Fact Sheet and the AMA 2026 PFS overview.

CMS also clarified that many of these services, when furnished in FQHCs and RHCs, are treated as care‑management or care‑coordination services and are typically reported under the general care‑management code G0511 rather than individual CPT codes. This aligns FQHC/RHC payment policy with the broader care‑management framework and allows clinics to bundle CCM, BHI, RPM/RTM‑like work, and certain new social‑needs services into a single monthly payment. For details, see the CodingIntel RHC/FQHC update and the CMS Physician Fee Schedule page.

For remote care and behavioral‑health integration, 2026 introduces shorter‑duration RPM and RTM codes for partial‑month engagement, modestly higher rates for core management codes, and clearer guidance on stacking with CCM, PCM, APCM, and SDOH/navigation G‑codes. Together, these changes signal CMS’s shift toward a more integrated, tech‑enabled care‑management ecosystem where longitudinal medical, behavioral, and social‑needs work is explicitly recognized and reimbursed. Helpful summaries: Medsien – 2026 Remote Care Changes, Prevounce – 2026 PFS FAQs, ChartSpan – 2026 PFS Care‑Management Highlights.

What is RPM?
Remote Physiologic Monitoring (RPM) uses connected digital devices to collect patient vitals outside of office visits and supports ongoing management of chronic or acute conditions. It is billed using a family of time‑based codes for setup, device supply, and clinical management time under the Medicare Physician Fee Schedule (PFS). Key 2026 changes introduce shorter time increments and partial‑month options for both device supply and management, making RPM easier to use when engagement is variable. See the CMS and expert summaries for details: CMS 2026 PFS Fact Sheet, RPMlogix 2026 Final Rule overview, Prevounce 2026 RPM FAQs.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patients with at least one acute or chronic condition where physiologic data (e.g., blood pressure, weight, pulse oximetry, glucose) will inform treatment decisions.
- A patient‑specific order for RPM and a documented care plan from the billing practitioner.
- Use of a medical‑grade device that automatically collects and transmits data, supplied by the practice or a qualified vendor.
- Required number of calendar days with device transmissions in a 30‑day period: 16 or more days for traditional device supply code 99454, and 2–15 days for the new partial‑month device code 99445 starting in 2026.
- RPM time and activities must be distinct from other billed care management services (e.g., CCM, PCM, TCM) in the same month; time cannot be double‑counted. Helpful discussions of these requirements: EHI 2026 RPM Updates, RPMlogix 2026 Final Rule, Prevounce 2026 FAQs.
Who May Bill?
RPM services can be billed by physicians and qualified health care professionals (such as NPs and PAs), with many day‑to‑day activities furnished by clinical staff under general supervision when incident‑to rules are met. Only one practitioner may bill RPM device and management codes for a given patient in a calendar month. Device supply must be billed by the entity that owns the device; independent diagnostic testing facilities (IDTFs) generally cannot bill RPM codes. See: EHI RPM updates, RPMlogix guide.
Billing Structure
RPM supports a layered monthly billing structure:
  • One‑time setup and education (99453) – initial enrollment, device setup, and patient education.
  • Monthly device supply and data transmission – 99454 for 16–30 days of use in a 30‑day period, and starting in 2026, 99445 for 2–15 days of use to cover partial months or lower‑adherence periods.
  • Clinical management time – 99457 for the first 20 minutes of interactive communication and care management per calendar month; 99458 for each additional 20 minutes; and new 99470 for 10–19 minutes of management when the full 20‑minute threshold is not met.
  • Standalone data‑review option (99091) – 30 minutes of practitioner time reviewing physiologic data when not counted toward other RPM or care management codes.
Management minutes for 99457, 99458, and 99470 include reviewing data, adjusting the care plan, communicating with the patient or caregivers, and coordinating with other clinicians, as long as time is documented and not counted toward other services. For detailed code‑by‑code breakdowns, see: RPMlogix 2026 Final Rule summary, Prevounce RPM FAQs.
Workflow
1
Patient Onboarding
Identify eligible patients from chronic disease panels or recent admissions; obtain consent; order RPM; perform one‑time device setup and patient education on use, targets, and when the care team will intervene.
2
Data Collection
Patient uses the device at home; data are transmitted on at least 2–16+ days in the 30‑day period, depending on whether 99445 or 99454 will be billed.
3

Clinical staff and providers review incoming data, document 10, 20, or 40+ minutes of RPM management time, and adjust medications, thresholds, or visit cadence as needed.
4
Escalation & Follow‑up
Trigger outreach, telehealth, or in‑person visits for concerning trends; coordinate with CCM or APCM teams and specialists for longitudinal follow‑up.

What is RTM?
Remote Therapeutic Monitoring (RTM) tracks non‑physiologic data such as medication adherence, therapy device use, pain, and functional status using digital tools for patients with musculoskeletal, respiratory, or other qualifying conditions. RTM codes are similar to RPM but focus on therapeutic response and self‑reported outcomes rather than vitals. CMS’ 2026 PFS final rule refines RTM by adding partial‑month supply and management options and clarifying how RTM can be combined with other care management services. Helpful overviews: Telehealth Resource Center CY 2026 RPM/RTM update, Medsien 2026 remote care changes, Prevounce 2026 PFS FAQs.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patients with conditions (commonly musculoskeletal or respiratory) where therapeutic adherence, symptoms, or function can be monitored remotely with a device or app.
- An RTM device or software application is ordered by a physician, qualified health care professional, or eligible therapist and used to capture self‑reported or other non‑physiologic data relevant to treatment.
- Sufficient days of data transmission or logged use within a 30‑day period: traditionally 16 or more days for device/software supply codes, with new 2–15 day options in 2026 for partial‑month engagement.
- RTM time and activities must be distinct from RPM and other care management services; CMS generally expects a given condition to be monitored with either RPM or RTM, not both at once. See: Telehealth Resource Center, Medsien, Prevounce FAQs.
Who May Bill?
RTM codes may be billed by physicians, other qualified health care professionals (QHPs), and certain therapists (such as physical and occupational therapists) who order and oversee the service. Clinical staff or therapy personnel may carry out many RTM activities under appropriate supervision, but the billing provider is responsible for reviewing data and making treatment decisions. For details, see: Telehealth Resource Center CY 2026 RTM, Medsien RTM overview.
Billing Structure
RTM billing is structured similarly to RPM but focused on therapeutic outcomes:
  • Initial setup and education (98975) – onboarding to the RTM device/app, patient education, and initial configuration.
  • Monthly device/software supply – 98976 for 16–30 days of monitored use per 30‑day period, and new 98985 (2026) for 2–15 days of use to support partial‑month engagement.
  • Management time – 98980 for the first 20 or more minutes of RTM management and interactive communication per calendar month; 98981 for each additional 20 minutes.
  • New short‑duration management code (98979) – 10–19 minutes of RTM management when the full 20‑minute threshold is not reached in 2026.
Time counted toward RTM management must involve reviewing RTM data, assessing adherence or response, communicating with the patient or caregivers, and adjusting treatment based on findings. Time cannot be double‑counted with other billed services. Code‑level explanations appear in: Telehealth Resource Center RTM webinar, Prevounce FAQs.
Workflow
1
Onboarding
Identify eligible patients (e.g., post‑surgical MSK, chronic low back pain, COPD/asthma), obtain consent, prescribe the RTM app/device, and train the patient on logging symptoms, activity, and adherence.
2
Tracking Period
Patient records daily or scheduled therapy activity, symptoms, and medication use over 2–30 days per month, creating a dataset for trend analysis and adherence monitoring.
3
Review & Support
Clinician or therapist reviews RTM data for 10, 20, or 40+ minutes per month, documents interventions and coaching, adjusts the plan or home exercise program, and escalates when needed (e.g., flares, non‑response).

What is CCM?
Chronic Care Management (CCM) supports Medicare patients with multiple chronic conditions that require regular monitoring, medication management, and coordination across providers. CCM is a non–face‑to‑face, time‑based service billed monthly when a care team furnishes structured management activities and maintains a comprehensive care plan. CMS created CCM to pay for the “between visit” work that primary care and specialty teams perform for complex patients. Strong overviews: CMS CCM FAQ, ChartSpan – What is CCM?, AMA CCM explainer.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare beneficiaries with two or more chronic conditions expected to last at least 12 months (or until death).
- Conditions must place the patient at significant risk of death, acute exacerbation/decompensation, or functional decline.
- A comprehensive, person‑centered care plan must be established, implemented, and revised as needed; it should be documented in a certified health record and shared with the patient and/or caregiver.
- Patient must provide informed consent (verbal or written, documented in the record), with an understanding that only one practitioner can bill CCM per month. See: CMS CCM FAQ, ChartSpan CCM guide.
Who May Bill?
CCM may be billed by physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, clinical nurse specialists, and certain other qualified health care professionals who provide ongoing care management for the patient. Most CCM work can be furnished by clinical staff under general supervision, with the billing practitioner directing care and remaining accountable for the care plan. Only one practitioner can bill CCM for a given patient in a calendar month, although CCM may often be billed in conjunction with RPM, BHI, or other services when time and requirements are distinct. Details: CMS CCM FAQ, AMA CCM.
Billing Structure
CCM uses a tiered, time‑based structure:[1]
  • 99490 – Standard CCM: at least 20 minutes of clinical staff time directed by a physician or other QHP, per calendar month.
  • 99439 – Add‑on CCM: each additional 20 minutes of clinical staff time (used with 99490 in the same month).
  • 99487 – Complex CCM: at least 60 minutes of clinical staff time with moderate/high medical decision‑making and care plan revision.
  • 99489 – Add‑on complex CCM: each additional 30 minutes of clinical staff time in a complex CCM month.
  • 99491 – CCM by practitioner: at least 30 minutes of CCM personally provided by a physician or QHP (no clinical staff time counted).
Time can include phone calls, portal messages, medication review and reconciliation, coordination with other clinicians and community services, and care‑plan updates, provided it is documented and not counted toward other time‑based services. Good code‑level summaries: ChartSpan 2026 PFS CCM analysis, CMS Physician Fee Schedule look‑up.
Workflow
1
Enrollment
Identify eligible patients from panels or risk lists, discuss CCM benefits and cost‑sharing, obtain consent, and build an initial comprehensive care plan with problem list, goals, and self‑management tasks.
2
Monthly Management
Clinical staff conduct structured outreach, medication checks, symptom assessments, and care‑gap closure activities, documenting cumulative minutes toward 99490/99439 or 99487/99489.
3
Care Plan Review & Escalation
The billing practitioner reviews CCM notes, updates the care plan, and triggers RPM, RTM, TCM, APCM, or in‑person visits when red flags appear.

What is PCM?
Principal Care Management (PCM) is designed for Medicare patients who have a single serious or high‑risk chronic condition that requires focused, ongoing management by a specialist or primary care clinician. PCM pays for non–face‑to‑face care management activities—similar to CCM—but centered on one driving condition (for example, advanced heart failure, severe asthma, or complex diabetes).[1] PCM is often used when a particular specialist or primary care clinician is primarily responsible for managing that condition over a period of at least three months.[1] Helpful descriptions: AMA Principal Care Management overview, ChartSpan PCM guide.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare beneficiaries with one serious chronic condition (or one main condition being managed by that clinician) expected to last at least three months.[1]
- The condition places the patient at significant risk of hospitalization, acute exacerbation, decompensation, or functional decline.[1]
- The managing clinician must develop and update a disease‑specific care plan, coordinate medications and services, and provide ongoing management.[1]
- Patient consent is required, and only one clinician may bill PCM for a given patient and month; PCM generally is not billed when that same clinician is also billing CCM for that patient in that month.[1] See: CMS CCM/PCM FAQ, AMA PCM.
Who May Bill?
PCM may be billed by physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and other qualified health care professionals who are managing the patient’s qualifying condition.[1] Clinical staff can furnish many PCM activities under general supervision, with their time counted toward PCM minutes when incident‑to requirements are met. Only one practitioner may bill PCM for a patient per month, and in most cases that practitioner should be the clinician “primarily responsible” for managing the principal condition. Sources: AMA PCM guidance, ChartSpan PCM billing.
Billing Structure
PCM uses time‑based codes that mirror CCM but apply to a single condition.[1] Under the 2026 PFS, the primary physician/QHP‑billed PCM codes are:
  • 99426 – PCM, 30 minutes of clinical staff time directed by a physician or other QHP, per calendar month.
  • 99427 – PCM add‑on, each additional 30 minutes of clinical staff time in the same month.
Time includes medication management, coordination with other clinicians, patient/caregiver communication, and care‑plan adjustments related to the principal condition, provided that it is documented and not counted toward CCM or other time‑based services for that patient in the same month. Good breakdowns: ChartSpan PCM guide, CMS PFS look‑up.
Workflow
1
Assessment & Enrollment
Identify patients with a single serious high‑risk condition (e.g., advanced CHF, brittle diabetes, severe COPD) where your clinic is the primary manager; obtain consent; and build a focused care plan that documents goals, medications, and monitoring needs.
2
Monthly PCM Management
Clinical staff conduct structured outreach, symptom and adherence checks, med reconciliation, and coordination with other clinicians, documenting at least 30 minutes per month for 99426 and additional 30‑minute increments for 99427 as needed.
3
Care Review & Transition
The billing clinician reviews PCM notes, adjusts treatment, and determines whether the patient should remain in PCM, transition into CCM when multiple conditions dominate, or layer RPM/RTM for closer monitoring.

What is TCM?
Transitional Care Management (TCM) services support Medicare patients as they transition from hospital or other facility settings back to the community. TCM covers the 30 days post‑discharge and includes medication reconciliation, timely face‑to‑face follow‑up, and coordination with other providers to reduce readmissions. TCM is billed with CPT codes 99495 and 99496, which differ based on the timing of the follow‑up visit and the complexity of medical decision‑making. See: CMS TCM Fact Sheet, AAFP – Transitional Care Management.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare beneficiaries discharged from certain eligible settings: inpatient acute‑care hospital, inpatient psychiatric hospital, long‑term care hospital, skilled nursing facility, inpatient rehabilitation facility, or hospital outpatient observation / partial hospitalization.[1]
- Patient must be returning to their community setting (home, domiciliary, rest home, or assisted living), not to another inpatient facility.[1]
- The billing practitioner assumes responsibility for TCM during the 30‑day post‑discharge period and provides or supervises required non–face‑to‑face services and a face‑to‑face visit within 7 or 14 days.[1]
- TCM cannot overlap with certain other services; generally, only one practitioner can bill TCM per beneficiary per 30‑day post‑discharge period.[1] See: CMS TCM Fact Sheet.
Who May Bill?
TCM can be billed by physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, clinical nurse specialists, and certain other qualified health care professionals who have appropriate Medicare billing privileges.[1] Clinical staff under the direction of the billing practitioner may furnish many of the non–face‑to‑face services (such as phone calls, coordination, and scheduling) under general supervision. Only one practitioner may bill TCM for a given discharge episode, and TCM generally should not be billed in the same month as CCM/PCM for the same practitioner and patient unless local policy clearly allows and time is distinct. See: CMS TCM Fact Sheet.
Billing Structure
TCM uses two CPT codes that differ by timing and decision‑making complexity:[1]
  • 99495 – TCM with moderate complexity: - Communication (direct contact, telephone, or electronic) with the patient/caregiver within 2 business days of discharge. - Medical decision‑making of at least moderate complexity during the service period. - Face‑to‑face visit within 14 calendar days of discharge.
  • 99496 – TCM with high complexity: - Same communication requirement within 2 business days of discharge. - Medical decision‑making of high complexity. - Face‑to‑face visit within 7 calendar days of discharge.
The 30‑day TCM period begins on the date of discharge and extends through day 29. All required non–face‑to‑face and face‑to‑face services must be documented to support the billed code. For detailed examples, see: CMS MLN TCM guide.
Workflow
1
Outreach & Discharge Review
Receive discharge notification, review the discharge summary and medication list, and contact the patient or caregiver within 2 business days to assess needs and schedule the follow‑up visit.
2
Face‑to‑Face Visit
Complete the TCM visit within 7 days (99496) or 14 days (99495), perform medication reconciliation, assess symptoms and functional status, and update the care plan accordingly.
3
Ongoing Coordination (30 days)
Provide ongoing phone/portal follow‑up, coordinate tests and referrals, monitor adherence and red flags, and hand off into CCM, PCM, RPM, or APCM as appropriate before day 30.

What is APCM?
Advanced Primary Care Management (APCM) represents CMS’ move toward team‑based, longitudinal primary care models that bundle chronic care, preventive services, behavioral health, and social‑needs support into a more comprehensive payment.[1][2] In the 2026 PFS, this concept is operationalized through enhanced care‑management G‑codes (such as G0511 for FQHCs/RHCs and new/expanded G‑codes for community health integration, principal illness navigation, and peer support) that sit on top of core primary care services.[1][2][3] APCM‑style models aim to pay practices for continuous management of high‑risk populations rather than just individual visits, aligning with ACO and advanced primary care initiatives. See: Hall Render 2026 PFS highlights, ChartSpan 2026 PFS summary.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patients attributed to a participating primary care practice or FQHC/RHC that is using the new or existing care‑management G‑codes as part of an advanced primary care or ACO‑aligned model.[1][2]
- Typically higher‑risk or complex patients who benefit from multidisciplinary care teams, community health workers, and navigation support (e.g., multiple chronic conditions plus social‑needs barriers).[2][3]
- A comprehensive care plan and team‑based follow‑up structure must be in place, with roles for clinicians, community health workers, navigators, and peer support specialists as defined by the relevant G‑codes (G0019, G0022, G0023, G0024, G0140, etc.).[2]
- The practice must meet documentation and supervision requirements associated with each code and applicable advanced payment model (e.g., ACO REACH, MSSP ACO, or other APCM programs).[1][3] Sources: Hall Render 2026 overview, ChartSpan.
Who May Bill?
APCM‑related codes can be billed by primary care physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and FQHC/RHC practitioners who meet the requirements for each specific G‑code.[2][3] For community health integration and principal illness navigation codes (G0019, G0022, G0023, G0024), billing practitioners oversee services furnished by community health workers and navigators under general supervision.[2] Peer support services (e.g., G0140 and related codes) allow practices to integrate trained peers into the care team, with billing practitioners responsible for oversight and integration of their work into the care plan.[2] Many APCM‑style payments in FQHC/RHC settings are ultimately billed under G0511 as a “general care management” umbrella, with APCM and ACO frameworks determining attribution and performance incentives.[3][4] See: CodingIntel RHC/FQHC update, CMS Physician Fee Schedule.
Billing Structure
APCM uses a combination of longitudinal care‑management payments and targeted G‑codes for social‑needs and navigation work:[1][2][3]
  • G0511 – General care management (FQHC/RHC): monthly comprehensive care management payment that can encompass CCM, BHI, and other care‑management services for FQHC/RHC settings.
  • G0019 – Community health integration, 60 minutes: services by community health workers addressing social determinants of health, care coordination, and resource linkage.
  • G0022 – Community health integration, additional 30 minutes: add‑on to G0019 when more time is needed.
  • G0023 – Principal illness navigation, 60 minutes: navigation and support for patients with serious conditions by a principal illness navigator.
  • G0024 – Principal illness navigation, additional 30 minutes: add‑on to G0023 for additional time.
  • G0140 – Peer support navigation, 60 minutes: peer specialist services focused on engagement, self‑management support, and care navigation (with add‑on codes for extra time in some models).
These codes are stacked on top of usual primary care E/M and care‑management services and, in APCM/ACO contexts, may be further blended with prospective or performance‑based payments. Good breakdowns of the new and expanded G‑codes: Hall Render 2026 PFS highlights, ChartSpan.

1

3

Integrate SDOH screening and interventions, embed BHI/CoCM where indicated, and use navigators and peers to overcome barriers to adherence, access, and follow‑up.

What is AWV?
The Medicare Annual Wellness Visit (AWV) is a yearly preventive service that includes a structured health risk assessment, review of medical and family history, risk‑factor screening, and development or update of a personalized prevention plan.[1] AWVs are billed with HCPCS codes G0438 (initial AWV) and G0439 (subsequent AWV) and are covered at 100% under Part B when requirements are met.[1][2] Under recent rules, AWVs can also serve as a touchpoint for standardized social drivers of health (SDOH) screening using HCPCS G0136 when clinically appropriate.[3] See: CMS AWV Quick Reference, CMS Preventive Services.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare Part B beneficiaries who have been enrolled for at least 12 months.[1]
- Not within 12 months of an Initial Preventive Physical Examination (IPPE, G0402) or another AWV (G0438/G0439).[1]
- The initial AWV (G0438) can be billed once per lifetime; subsequent AWVs (G0439) are allowed once every 12 months.[1]
- A standardized SDOH risk assessment (G0136) may be performed in conjunction with the AWV or other qualifying E/M or behavioral health services, generally not more than once every 6 months.[3] Details: CMS AWV chart.
Who May Bill?
AWVs may be billed by physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, clinical nurse specialists, and certain other practitioners allowed by CMS.[1] Clinical staff can complete many components of the health risk assessment and screening under appropriate supervision, but the billing practitioner is responsible for reviewing findings, performing required components, and finalizing the personalized prevention plan.[1][2] When an SDOH risk assessment (G0136) is performed in conjunction with an AWV, it is typically billed by the same practitioner if requirements are met, with cost sharing generally waived when modifier‑33 is appended.[3] See: CMS SDOH Risk Assessment guidance (SE24006).
Billing Structure
The core AWV billing elements are:[1][3]
  • G0438 – Initial AWV: once per lifetime, includes a comprehensive health risk assessment, review of medical and family history, list of current providers and medications, measurements, detection of cognitive impairment, and development of a written personalized prevention plan.
  • G0439 – Subsequent AWV: annual visit that updates the health risk assessment and personalized prevention plan and reviews risk factors, functional status, and medications.
  • G0136 – SDOH Risk Assessment: standardized risk‑assessment instrument (5–15 minutes) identifying social‑risk factors; can be billed with AWV, other E/M, or behavioral health visits no more than once every 6 months.[3]
AWVs are typically furnished without cost‑sharing to the patient, and when G0136 is provided as a preventive service with modifier‑33, cost sharing is generally waived as well.[3] Guidance: CMS AWV Quick Reference, CMS SDOH G0136 memo.
Workflow
1

Prior to the visit, collect the health risk assessment (HRA), including medical and family history, medication list, self‑reported functional status, and basic SDOH questions, via portal, phone, or paper forms.
2

During the AWV, confirm and complete the HRA, perform screenings (cognitive, depression, fall risk, etc.), review medications, reconcile providers, and—when indicated—administer a standardized SDOH risk assessment (G0136).
3

Generate or update the personalized prevention plan, including recommended screenings, immunizations, lifestyle interventions, and referrals, and share it with the patient and caregivers.
4
Care Mgmt Handoff
Route high‑risk patients or those with unmet needs into CCM, PCM, RPM/RTM, APCM, BHI/CoCM, or social‑services workflows for ongoing follow‑up.

What are BHI & CoCM?
General Behavioral Health Integration (BHI, CPT 99484) is a monthly care‑management service for patients with one or more mental, behavioral, or psychiatric conditions, providing at least 20 minutes of behaviorally focused care by clinical staff under the direction of a billing practitioner. It includes systematic assessment, care‑plan development, monitoring, and coordination of behavioral health treatment in primary care or similar settings. Psychiatric Collaborative Care Model (CoCM, CPT 99492–99494 and HCPCS G2214) is a higher‑intensity team‑based model in which a behavioral health care manager and psychiatric consultant work with the treating practitioner using a registry and structured case reviews. Both BHI and CoCM are designated Behavioral Health Integration services under Medicare.[1][2] Key references: CMS MLN909432 – Behavioral Health Integration Services, ThoroughCare 99484 overview, APA – Getting Paid in the Collaborative Care Model.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patients with at least one identified mental, behavioral, or psychiatric condition (including substance use disorders) that can be managed in primary care or collaboratively with behavioral health specialists.[1][3]
- The condition may be pre‑existing or newly diagnosed by the billing practitioner, based on clinical evaluation and, ideally, validated rating scales (e.g., PHQ‑9, GAD‑7).[1][3]
- The patient requires ongoing assessment, active care‑plan management, and coordination beyond intermittent visits; self‑limited or very mild symptoms typically do not justify BHI/CoCM.[1][3]
- BHI is generally appropriate for mild–moderate or stable behavioral health needs managed largely within primary care, while CoCM is more appropriate for moderate–severe, complex, or treatment‑resistant conditions where psychiatric consultation is needed.[1][2] Detailed criteria: CMS BHI MLN, Proem 99484 overview.
Who May Bill?
BHI and CoCM can be billed by physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, clinical nurse specialists, and certain other qualified health care professionals who provide ongoing primary or specialty care for the patient.[1] For BHI (99484), clinical staff under general supervision can furnish most care‑management activities, with the billing practitioner overseeing the plan; a behavioral health care manager is optional but often helpful.[1][4] For CoCM (99492–99494/G2214), the billing practitioner must direct a care team that includes: a behavioral health care manager (e.g., clinical social worker, nurse, or psychologist) and a psychiatric consultant who provides regular case‑review and treatment recommendations, usually via a registry‑based caseload.[1][2] Only one practitioner may bill BHI or CoCM codes for a given patient per month, though these codes may often be billed in conjunction with CCM or other care‑management services when time and requirements are distinct.[1][4] See: CMS BHI FAQ, AAFP BHI coding resource.
Billing Structure
BHI (CPT 99484)
- Monthly care‑management service with at least 20 minutes of clinical staff time focused on behavioral health, directed by the billing practitioner.[1][3][4]
- Includes: systematic assessment using validated rating scales, care‑plan development and revision, proactive follow‑up, coordination with mental‑health and medical providers, and patient/caregiver support.[1][5]

CoCM (CPT 99492–99494, HCPCS G2214)
- 99492 – Initial psychiatric collaborative care management in the first month, first 70 minutes of behavioral health care‑manager time in consultation with a psychiatric consultant, under the direction of the treating practitioner.[1][2]
- 99493 – Subsequent months, first 60 minutes of care‑manager time with psychiatric consultant and treating practitioner involvement.[1][2]
- 99494 – Add‑on code for each additional 30 minutes of care‑manager activities in any month (billed with 99492 or 99493).[1][2]
- G2214 – 30‑minute CoCM code for initial or subsequent months when a shorter amount of time is furnished, with the same team and registry requirements.[5][6]

All CoCM codes require: (1) A patient registry or tracking system for the CoCM caseload, (2) Regular (typically weekly) systematic case review between the behavioral health care manager and psychiatric consultant, and (3) Documentation of care‑plan adjustments and communication back to the treating practitioner.[1][2] Helpful coding and payment guides: IPRO BHI/CoCM Coding Guide, CodingIntel CoCM in RHC/FQHC.
Workflow
1
Identify & Enroll
Use screening tools (e.g., PHQ‑9, GAD‑7, AUDIT‑C) and referral cues to identify patients with behavioral health needs; confirm diagnosis; explain BHI or CoCM; and obtain consent, documenting enrollment and initial rating scales.[1][5]
2
Assessment & Care Plan
Perform structured behavioral health assessment, including symptom severity, functional impact, and comorbidities; develop a care plan that integrates psychiatric and medical goals, and enter the patient into a registry (required for CoCM).[1][2][5]
3
Ongoing Management
Provide follow‑up contacts (phone, video, portal), track rating scales, monitor medication adherence and side effects, coordinate with external therapists, and document time toward 99484 (≥20 minutes) or CoCM thresholds (99492/99493/99494 or G2214).[1][3][4]
4
Case Review & Escalation
For CoCM, hold regular registry‑based case review sessions between the behavioral health care manager and psychiatric consultant, update treatment recommendations, and escalate or step‑down care (e.g., to specialty psychiatry or BHI‑only) based on response.[1][2]

What is SDOH Screening & Intervention?
Social Drivers of Health (SDOH) services in Medicare focus on systematically identifying and addressing social‑risk factors—such as food insecurity, housing instability, transportation barriers, and utility insecurity—that affect health outcomes and care access. CMS now pays separately for standardized SDOH risk assessment using HCPCS G0136 when performed with certain visits, and has created additional G‑codes in 2026 for community health integration and navigation that can be used to deliver and document SDOH‑focused interventions.[1][2] These services are meant to be integrated with AWV, primary‑care visits, behavioral health, and care‑management programs rather than offered in isolation. See: CMS SE24006 – SDOH Risk Assessment G0136, Hall Render 2026 PFS highlights.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare beneficiaries receiving eligible E/M, behavioral health, or preventive services (including AWV) where a standardized SDOH risk assessment is appropriate.[1]
- Patients for whom the clinician believes SDOH screening will meaningfully inform the care plan or referrals, especially those with chronic disease, frequent utilization, or known social‑risk indicators.[1][3]
- For ongoing SDOH‑focused care management and navigation services (e.g., community health integration, principal illness navigation), patients typically have documented social‑risk needs and clinical complexity benefiting from longitudinal follow‑up.[2][3]
- Frequency limits and documentation requirements must be followed (for example, G0136 no more than once every 6 months, unless policy is updated).[1] See: CMS SE24006.
Who May Bill?
SDOH risk assessment (G0136) can be billed by physicians, NPs, PAs, clinical nurse specialists, and other practitioners who can bill E/M or covered preventive/behavioral health services when they furnish or supervise the assessment as part of an eligible encounter.[1] Community health integration (CHI) and principal illness navigation (PIN) G‑codes (e.g., G0019, G0022, G0023, G0024) can be billed by the supervising practitioner while services are delivered by trained community health workers or navigators under general supervision.[2][3] In FQHCs and RHCs, many SDOH‑related services are billed through general care‑management G‑codes (such as G0511), with the SDOH elements documented in the medical record and supporting APCM or ACO arrangements.[2][4] References: CodingIntel RHC/FQHC update, CMS PFS page.
Billing Structure
SDOH‑related services in 2026 fall into two main categories:[1][2][3]
  • SDOH Risk Assessment (G0136)
    - 5–15 minutes using a standardized, evidence‑based instrument to identify social‑risk factors.
    - Can be billed with AWV (G0438/G0439), many E/M visits, and behavioral health services when requirements are met, typically no more than once every 6 months.
    - When furnished as a preventive service with modifier‑33, beneficiary cost‑sharing is generally waived.
  • SDOH‑Focused Care Management & Navigation
    - G0019 – Community health integration, initial 60 minutes per month, addressing SDOH‑related barriers via assessment, coaching, and linkage to resources.
    - G0022 – Each additional 30 minutes of community health integration time per month.
    - G0023 – Principal illness navigation, initial 60 minutes per month for patients with serious conditions needing intensive navigation and support.
    - G0024 – Each additional 30 minutes of principal illness navigation time per month.
    - These codes are often used in FQHC/RHC and APCM/ACO settings and may roll up to G0511 for payment while supporting richer documentation of SDOH work.
Code‑level details: Hall Render 2026 PFS summary, ChartSpan 2026 PFS analysis.
Workflow
1
Screen & Identify
Use a standardized SDOH tool during AWV, primary‑care, or behavioral health encounters to screen for food, housing, transportation, utilities, interpersonal safety, and other key domains; document results and bill G0136 when criteria are met.
2
Stratify & Refer
Stratify patients by SDOH risk level; create referrals to social‑service agencies, community‑based organizations, and internal CHW/navigator teams for those with high or multiple needs.
3
Ongoing CHW/Navigator Support
Community health workers or navigators provide follow‑up calls, resource navigation, appointment support, and benefit applications, tracking time toward G0019/G0022 and G0023/G0024 as appropriate.
4
Integration with Care Mgmt
Close the loop by feeding SDOH findings and interventions into CCM, RPM/RTM, APCM, and BHI/CoCM workflows so that medical and social‑risk work is coordinated and visible to the whole team.

What is GDMT Optimization?
Guideline‑Directed Medical Therapy (GDMT) refers to the evidence‑based drug and device regimens recommended by specialty societies (e.g., ACC/AHA/HFSA, GOLD, ADA) for conditions such as heart failure, ischemic heart disease, COPD/asthma, and diabetes.[1][2] In Medicare, there is no single “GDMT code,” but care‑management, remote monitoring, and behavioral‑health codes (CCM, PCM, RPM/RTM, BHI/CoCM, APCM) can be used to support systematic GDMT initiation, titration, and adherence across a population.[3][4] This module focuses on how to operationalize GDMT within those existing payment structures rather than introducing new CPT/HCPCS codes. Key references: 2022 ACC/AHA/HFSA Heart Failure Guideline, ADA Standards of Care.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patients with chronic conditions for which strong guideline‑directed regimens exist (e.g., HFrEF, ASCVD, T2DM, CKD, COPD/asthma, secondary prevention post‑MI).[1][2]
- Suboptimal GDMT relative to guidelines (e.g., missing classes, under‑titrated doses, contraindications not documented, poor adherence) identified via registry or chart review.[1][3]
- Willingness to engage in medication adjustments, monitoring, and follow‑up; often already enrolled or eligible for CCM, PCM, RPM/RTM, APCM, or BHI/CoCM.[3][4]
- For heart‑failure‑focused GDMT programs, patients with HFrEF (LVEF ≤40%) and recent hospitalization, ED visit, or persistent symptoms are prime candidates.[1][5] See: ACC/AHA/HFSA HF guideline, KFF Medicare & chronic disease.
Who May Bill?
GDMT optimization is billed through existing codes rather than a unique GDMT code. Primary care clinicians, cardiologists, endocrinologists, pulmonologists, and other specialists can use CCM, PCM, RPM/RTM, BHI/CoCM, APCM, and TCM codes when they meet each program’s requirements.[3][4] Clinical pharmacists, nurses, and care‑management staff can furnish much of the GDMT work (medication titration under protocol, education, adherence support) under general supervision, with time counted toward the relevant care‑management codes.[3][4] Team‑based GDMT programs should explicitly assign billing responsibility (e.g., CCM + RPM under PCP, PCM under cardiology) to avoid duplicate billing and align with attribution in ACO/APCM models.[3] For payment policy context: AMA 2026 MPFS Summary, CMS CY 2026 PFS Fact Sheet.
Billing Structure (Using Existing Codes)
GDMT work can be wrapped into existing programs as follows:[3][4][5]
  • CCM / Complex CCM – multi‑morbid patients with multiple GDMT‑eligible conditions (HF, diabetes, CKD, COPD) where monthly non–face‑to‑face management supports multi‑drug titration and adherence.
  • PCM – single dominant condition (e.g., HFrEF or brittle T2DM) where one clinician is chiefly responsible for intensifying and monitoring GDMT.
  • RPM / RTM – physiologic (e.g., BP, weight, O2 sat) or therapeutic response monitoring during GDMT initiation and titration (e.g., HF or hypertension RPM, inhaler‑use RTM for COPD/asthma).
  • BHI / CoCM – when behavioral health conditions or adherence barriers complicate GDMT; BH integration supports depression/anxiety management, motivational interviewing, and substance‑use treatment.
  • APCM & SDOH G‑codes – community health workers, navigators, and peers can help with cost barriers, transportation, and social‑needs factors that undermine GDMT adherence (G0019, G0022, G0023, G0024, G0140).
Each GDMT encounter (med titration call, lab follow‑up, remote monitoring review) is documented and the time is allocated to the appropriate care‑management or monitoring codes according to program rules rather than billed as “GDMT” directly. Overviews of how chronic disease management codes support guideline‑based care: ChartSpan 2026 PFS summary, Prevounce 2026 PFS FAQs.
Workflow
1
Identify Gaps
Build condition‑specific registries (e.g., HFrEF, T2DM, ASCVD) and compare current meds, doses, and labs against guideline recommendations to flag missing classes, under‑dosing, or lack of documented contraindications.
2
Plan & Enroll
For each flagged patient, determine the right program (CCM vs PCM vs APCM), obtain consent where required, and create a GDMT‑focused care plan with target meds, titration steps, monitoring labs, and symptom triggers.
3
Titrate & Monitor
Use CCM/PCM and RPM/RTM encounters to adjust doses at guideline‑recommended intervals, check vitals and labs, review side effects, and reinforce adherence; document time toward the appropriate codes each month.
4
Address Barriers & BH
For patients struggling with adherence, cost, or behavior change, leverage BHI/CoCM, CHW/navigator support, and SDOH G‑codes to resolve insurance, pharmacy, transportation, or mental‑health barriers that block GDMT.

APCM & SDOH G0019, G0022, G0023, G0024, G0140, G0511 Community health workers, navigators, and peers address social‑needs and navigation issues (cost, transportation, benefits) that interfere with guideline‑directed therapy.
Note: GDMT itself is not a separately billable code; instead, these codes provide the infrastructure to support safe titration, adherence, and barrier resolution at scale. Payment levels and stacking rules should be confirmed in the CY 2026 Physician Fee Schedule and payer policies.[3][4]
2026 Rule Notes
- The CY 2026 PFS final rule increases payment for several care‑management and remote monitoring codes (CCM, RPM/RTM, behavioral‑health integration) and exempts them from the new efficiency adjustment applied to many non‑time‑based services, strengthening the business case for GDMT‑oriented care‑management programs.[3][4][6]
- CMS expands SDOH‑ and navigation‑related G‑codes (community health integration, principal illness navigation, peer support), which can be directly integrated into GDMT workflows to address cost, access, and adherence barriers.[4][5]
- Specialty guidelines continue to emphasize early initiation and maximally tolerated dosing of GDMT (especially in HFrEF, ASCVD, and T2DM), and 2026 payment policy gives practices more tools to finance the longitudinal work required to reach those targets.[1][2][5]

Integrated Behavioral Health: Payment & Models

High-level reference for General BHI (99484), CoCM (99492–99494), HCPCS G0323, and APCM BH add-on codes (G0568–G0570): what they pay, who can bill, and which validated tools and assessments are required.

Program Stacking & Overlap Rules

Device programs (RPM/RTM), longitudinal care programs (CCM/PCM/APCM), transitional care (TCM), and behavioral health (BHI/CoCM) can often be billed together in the same month, but only when each program’s requirements are met independently, time is not double-counted, and CMS stacking exclusions are enforced.

Program Selection Decision Guide

Start by asking whether the clinic is participating in APCM. If yes, APCM becomes the base longitudinal program and you layer RPM/RTM, BHI/CoCM, and TCM as add-ons. If not, route into CCM (for 2+ chronic conditions) or PCM (for one serious condition), then add remote monitoring and behavioral health based on clinical need and team capacity.

Choosing Between BHI and CoCM

General BHI is a lower-intensity integration model (≥20 minutes/month) that can stand alone and fits mild–moderate behavioral health needs. CoCM is a higher-intensity model (60–70+ minutes/month) that requires a three-person team and registry-based case management, reserved for moderate–severe or complex behavioral health conditions.

Continuation Standards: BHI & CoCM

Apply continuation standards monthly so that programs match real clinical need: BHI continues while there is an active BH condition, validated scales in use, a living care plan, and ≥20 minutes of documented work; CoCM continues only while the full team and registry are active and 60–70+ minutes of care manager time remain necessary.

Patient Archetypes & Optimal Program Mix

Use these archetypes to design playbooks and pro formas: complex primary care patient (APCM + RPM + CoCM), multi-morbid patient (complex CCM + RPM + BHI), single high-risk condition (PCM + RTM + BHI), mild BH needs (CCM regular + RPM + BHI), and severe BH needs (complex CCM + RPM + CoCM).

Team Requirements by Program

Supports staffing decisions by clarifying required vs optional roles across programs: RPM (billing provider + clinical staff + device specialist), RTM (billing provider/therapist + therapy staff), CCM/PCM (billing provider + clinical staff), APCM (multidisciplinary primary care team), TCM (billing provider + clinical staff + case manager), BHI (billing provider + clinical staff), and CoCM (required triad of provider, BH care manager, psychiatric consultant).

CCM Service Levels: Regular vs Complex

Regular CCM fits patients with 2+ chronic conditions, 20–40 minutes of monthly non-face-to-face work, and low–moderate medical decision making. Complex CCM is appropriate when you consistently spend ≥60 minutes and decision-making is moderate–high with multiple specialists, frequent exacerbations, and high coordination needs.
