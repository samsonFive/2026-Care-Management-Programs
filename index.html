2026 CMS Care Management Program Selector

Care Management programs let teams bill Medicare for structured coordination and ongoing support for patients with chronic or acute needs. These codes support remote monitoring, behavioral health management, transition support, and primary care under the CY 2026 Physician Fee Schedule.
CY 2026 Care Management Overview

Beginning January 1, 2026, Medicare finalized a broad set of updates to care management under the Physician Fee Schedule. Time‑based services such as RPM, RTM, CCM, behavioral health integration, and other care management codes are exempt from the new efficiency adjustment that reduces work RVUs for many non‑time‑based services, helping protect longitudinal care revenues even as other services face downward pressure.[web:29][web:147][web:157]

CMS also clarified that services designated as care management will be treated as care coordination services for FQHCs and RHCs, aligning payment policy across settings and tying many of these services into the general care management code G0511.[web:29][web:149] In parallel, virtual direct supervision and expanded telehealth flexibilities—including removal of certain visit‑frequency limits—were made permanent, supporting remote care teams and digital‑first workflows.[web:147][web:150]

For remote care, 2026 introduces new short‑duration RPM and RTM codes for partial‑month engagement, higher rates for core management codes, and clearer stacking rules with CCM and other longitudinal programs, signaling CMS’s shift toward a more integrated, tech‑enabled care management ecosystem.[web:148][web:150][web:159]

What is AWV?

What are BHI & CoCM?
General Behavioral Health Integration (BHI, 99484) is a monthly care management service for patients with one or more mental, behavioral, or psychiatric conditions, providing at least 20 minutes of behaviorally focused care by clinical staff under the direction of a billing practitioner.[web:12][web:16][web:101] Psychiatric Collaborative Care Model (CoCM, 99492–99494, G2214) is a higher-intensity team-based model in which a behavioral health care manager and psychiatric consultant work with the treating practitioner using a registry and structured case reviews.[web:12][web:116] Both are classified by CMS as Behavioral Health Integration services and can be layered with other chronic care programs when requirements are independently met.[web:12][web:116]
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare patients with at least one identified mental, behavioral, or psychiatric condition, including substance use disorders.[web:12][web:164]
- Condition may be pre-existing or newly diagnosed by the billing practitioner.[web:12][web:164]
- Patient requires ongoing assessment, care planning, and coordination beyond a single visit.[web:12]
- BHI: appropriate for mild–moderate or stable behavioral health needs managed in primary care.[web:16][web:101]
- CoCM: appropriate for moderate–severe or complex behavioral health conditions where specialist input via a psychiatric consultant is needed.[web:12][web:116]
Who May Bill?
- BHI and CoCM can be billed by physicians, NPs, PAs, CNSs, and certain other qualified health care professionals who provide ongoing primary or specialty care.[web:12][web:21]
- For BHI, clinical staff under general supervision can furnish most care management activities; a behavioral health care manager is optional.[web:12][web:107]
- For CoCM, the billing practitioner must direct a team that includes a behavioral health care manager and a psychiatric consultant; their work is counted toward the required time.[web:12][web:116]
- Only one practitioner may bill BHI or CoCM codes for a patient per month, but codes can often be billed in conjunction with CCM when all requirements are separately met.[web:12][web:101][web:116]
Billing Structure
BHI (99484):
- Monthly care management service with ≥20 minutes of clinical staff time focused on behavioral health, directed by the billing practitioner.[web:12][web:16][web:101]
- Includes initial and ongoing assessment using validated rating scales, care plan development and revision, monitoring, and coordination of behavioral health treatment.[web:12][web:162]

CoCM (99492–99494, G2214):
- 99492: Initial month, first 70 minutes of behavioral health care manager activities in consultation with a psychiatric consultant and directed by the treating practitioner.[web:12][web:163]
- 99493: Subsequent months, first 60 minutes of care manager activities.[web:12][web:163]
- 99494: Add-on for each additional 30 minutes in any month (stackable up to CMS limits).[web:12][web:163]
- G2214: 30-minute CoCM code that can be used as an alternative base in some circumstances.[web:161][web:163]
- All CoCM codes require use of a patient registry, regular systematic case review with the psychiatric consultant, and documented care plan adjustments.[web:12][web:116]
Workflow
1
Identify & Enroll
Screen patients for depression, anxiety, or other BH conditions using validated tools (e.g., PHQ-9, GAD-7); confirm diagnosis; obtain consent for BHI or CoCM.[web:12][web:21]
2
Assessment & Care Plan
Perform structured behavioral health assessment, create or update a care plan integrated with medical problems, and document goals and interventions.[web:12][web:162]
3
Ongoing Management
Provide follow-up contacts, monitor symptoms using rating scales, adjust meds and therapies, coordinate referrals, and track time toward 99484 or CoCM thresholds.[web:12][web:16]
4
Case Review & Escalation
For CoCM, review caseload weekly with the psychiatric consultant using a registry; escalate care or step down based on severity and response.[web:12][web:116]

What is SDOH Risk Assessment?
The Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) Risk Assessment is a brief, standardized questionnaire that identifies health-related social needs such as food, housing, transportation, utilities, and safety. Medicare pays separately for this service under HCPCS G0136 when a validated, evidence-based tool is used and results are documented and acted upon.
Patient Eligibility
- Medicare beneficiaries in whom social needs may affect diagnosis, treatment, or adherence
- Outpatient or other qualifying settings when furnished with an E/M, behavioral health service, or AWV
- Assessment not performed more than once every 6 months per practitioner per beneficiary.
Who May Bill?
Physicians and other practitioners eligible to bill Medicare Part B may bill G0136 when they or their supervised clinical staff administer the assessment. Clinical staff (e.g., RN, MA, social worker, care manager) can deliver the tool under incident-to or applicable supervision rules, with the billing practitioner reviewing and incorporating results into the care plan.
Billing Structure
- G0136: Administration of standardized, evidence-based SDOH risk assessment, 5â€“15 minutes
- May be billed with AWV (G0438/G0439), E/M, or behavioral health visits when criteria are met
- Not more often than every 6 months per practitioner per beneficiary.

What is GDMT?
Guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) for heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) is a structured, evidence-based medication regimen built on four foundational drug classes that reduce mortality, HF hospitalizations, and improve quality of life. It combines reninâ€“angiotensin system inhibition (ACEI/ARB/ARNI), evidence-based beta-blockers, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRA), and SGLT2 inhibitors, titrated to guideline doses whenever possible.
Patient Eligibility
- Symptomatic HFrEF (LVEF â‰¤ 40%) without contraindications to core drug classes.
- Often extended to high-risk HF patients with recent decompensation, incomplete GDMT, or poor adherence.
- HFmrEF/HFpEF patients may benefit from selected components (especially SGLT2 inhibitors) based on guideline and local protocol.
- Ideal candidates for a GDMT Enablement Program are those with recurrent hospitalizations, complex regimens, or difficulty maintaining adherence.
Who May Manage?
GDMT initiation and titration are typically led by cardiologists, HF specialists, or primary care clinicians, often supported by clinical pharmacists and advanced practice providers. Remote/digital optimization programs use nurse care managers and HF titration teams who review RPM data and adherence signals, then escalate dose changes and medication adjustments to prescribing clinicians within protocol-driven pathways.
Clinical Components
- Confirm HF phenotype and EF; review current medications and contraindications.
- Initiate or optimize each GDMT â€œpillarâ€: ACEI/ARB/ARNI, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2 inhibitor, plus diuretic strategy and other indicated therapies (e.g., hydralazine/isosorbide in select patients).
- Monitor vitals (BP, HR), weight, renal function, electrolytes, and symptoms for tolerance and decompensation.

Integrated Behavioral Health: Payment & Models

High-level reference for General BHI (99484), CoCM (99492–99494), HCPCS G0323, and APCM BH add-on codes (G0568–G0570): what they pay, who can bill, and which validated tools and assessments are required.

Program Stacking & Overlap Rules

Device programs (RPM/RTM), longitudinal care programs (CCM/PCM/APCM), transitional care (TCM), and behavioral health (BHI/CoCM) can often be billed together in the same month, but only when each program’s requirements are met independently, time is not double-counted, and CMS stacking exclusions are enforced.

Revenue Stacking Examples

These waterfalls translate compliant stacks into approximate annual per-patient revenue for three core scenarios: complex primary care patient, multi-morbid patient, and single high-risk condition patient.

Program Selection Decision Guide

Start by asking whether the clinic is participating in APCM. If yes, APCM becomes the base longitudinal program and you layer RPM/RTM, BHI/CoCM, and TCM as add-ons. If not, route into CCM (for 2+ chronic conditions) or PCM (for one serious condition), then add remote monitoring and behavioral health based on clinical need and team capacity.

Choosing Between BHI and CoCM

General BHI is a lower-intensity integration model (≥20 minutes/month) that can stand alone and fits mild–moderate behavioral health needs. CoCM is a higher-intensity model (60–70+ minutes/month) that requires a three-person team and registry-based case management, reserved for moderate–severe or complex behavioral health conditions.

Continuation Standards: BHI & CoCM

Apply continuation standards monthly so that programs match real clinical need: BHI continues while there is an active BH condition, validated scales in use, a living care plan, and ≥20 minutes of documented work; CoCM continues only while the full team and registry are active and 60–70+ minutes of care manager time remain necessary.

Patient Archetypes & Optimal Program Mix

Use these archetypes to design playbooks and pro formas: complex primary care patient (APCM + RPM + CoCM), multi-morbid patient (complex CCM + RPM + BHI), single high-risk condition (PCM + RTM + BHI), mild BH needs (CCM regular + RPM + BHI), and severe BH needs (complex CCM + RPM + CoCM).

Team Requirements by Program

Supports staffing decisions by clarifying required vs optional roles across programs: RPM (billing provider + clinical staff + device specialist), RTM (billing provider/therapist + therapy staff), CCM/PCM (billing provider + clinical staff), APCM (multidisciplinary primary care team), TCM (billing provider + clinical staff + case manager), BHI (billing provider + clinical staff), and CoCM (required triad of provider, BH care manager, psychiatric consultant).

CCM Service Levels: Regular vs Complex

Regular CCM fits patients with 2+ chronic conditions, 20–40 minutes of monthly non-face-to-face work, and low–moderate medical decision making. Complex CCM is appropriate when you consistently spend ≥60 minutes and decision-making is moderate–high with multiple specialists, frequent exacerbations, and high coordination needs.
